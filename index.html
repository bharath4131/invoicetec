How to Write an Invoice: A Complete Guide for Freelancers

Published by InvoiceTec Editorial Team • Updated June 2026

Writing a professional invoice is critical to getting paid on time. An invoice is more than just a request for payment—it is a legal document and a reflection of your business's professionalism. In this guide, we break down exactly what should go on a standard invoice.

1. Essential Invoice Elements

Every professional invoice should contain these core components to ensure clarity and legal validity:

  • Branding: Your business name, logo, and contact info (email, phone, address).
  • Client Information: The client's business name, contact person, and billing address.
  • Invoice Number: A unique sequential identifier (e.g. INV-2026-001) for record-keeping.
  • Dates: The date the invoice was issued, and the exact payment due date.
  • Line Items: A detailed breakdown of the products or services provided, quantities, hourly rates, and total amounts.
  • Totals & Taxes: The subtotal, any discounts applied, tax percentages (VAT/GST/Sales Tax), and the final Total Due.

2. Tips for Faster Payments

  1. State Clear Payment Terms: Don't leave terms ambiguous. State "Due on Receipt" or use standard net terms like "Net 30".
  2. Itemize Your Work: Clients delay payments when they don't understand what they are paying for. Provide clear descriptions for each line item.
  3. Send Invoices Promptly: The faster you invoice, the faster you get paid. Draft your invoice as soon as the project milestone is reached.
  4. Provide Direct Payment Links: Include direct links to PayPal, Stripe, or bank details to minimize friction.

3. Generate Free Professional PDFs

Instead of manually compiling invoices in Word or Excel, you can use **InvoiceTec** to create, format, and download beautiful PDF invoices instantly. Your data stays 100% private and stored locally in your browser.

Standard Invoice Payment Terms: Net 30, Net 15 & COD Explained

Published by InvoiceTec Editorial Team • Updated June 2026

Selecting the correct payment terms determines how quickly cash flows into your business. Incorrect or ambiguous terms lead to client confusion and delayed payments. This guide explains standard invoicing terms and how to select the right ones.

1. Standard Invoicing Terms Glossary

Here are the most common payment terms used in business-to-business transactions:

  • Net 30 / Net 15: The payment is due 30 (or 15) calendar days from the invoice date. This is the most common standard term in corporate environments.
  • Due on Receipt: The invoice must be paid immediately upon receipt. Best for project milestones or creative deliveries.
  • PIA (Payment in Advance): Requires the client to pay the total fee before any work begins. Recommended for new clients or large capital requirements.
  • COD (Cash on Delivery): Payment is collected at the exact moment the service or product is delivered.
  • 2/10 Net 30: The client receives a 2% discount if they pay within 10 days; otherwise, the full balance is due in 30 days. This incentivizes fast payments.

2. How to Choose the Right Terms

When selecting payment terms for your invoice, consider the following rules of thumb:

  • For small clients: Prefer Due on Receipt or Net 15 to maintain healthy cash flow.
  • For corporate clients: Prepare for Net 30 or even Net 60, as large companies have structured accounts payable runs.
  • For upfront expenses: Always charge a deposit or use PIA (Payment in Advance) to cover your raw costs.
